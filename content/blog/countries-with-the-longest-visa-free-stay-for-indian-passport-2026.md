## Why "How Long" Matters More Than "Where"

Most lists tell Indian travellers *where* they can go without a full visa. Far fewer explain *how long* they can actually stay once they arrive — and that number is what decides whether a destination works for a two-week holiday, a month of slow travel, or a season of remote work. A country that waves you in but only for two weeks is very different from one that grants ninety days.

This guide ranks easy-access destinations for Indian passports by the length of stay typically permitted, using VisitPlane's dataset values as guidance. Treat every figure as a starting point to confirm, not a promise — permitted stays can change, and the days you actually receive can depend on the officer, the entry type, and current policy.

> **Key takeaway:** "Easy to enter" and "long to stay" are not the same thing. Always separate the *entry type* (visa-free, ETA, visa on arrival, or e-Visa) from the *maximum stay* — and confirm both for your exact trip before booking.

## Entry Type vs Stay Length: The Distinction

Four terms decide your experience at the border, and they are not interchangeable:

- **Visa-free** means no advance authorisation and no visa — you arrive and are admitted for a set period.
- **ETA (Electronic Travel Authorisation)** means a quick online approval before you fly, but not a full visa.
- **Visa on arrival (VOA)** means you obtain the visa at the airport or border on landing, usually for a fee.
- **e-Visa** means you apply and pay online in advance and travel with an approved document.

A 90-day e-Visa and a 90-day visa-free entry both give you three months, but the effort, cost and predictability differ. The tables below note the entry type alongside the stay so you can weigh convenience against duration.

## Nepal and Bhutan: The Special Cases

The two most generous arrangements for Indian passport holders don't fit any of the tables above, because they are not ordinary tourist-visa relationships — and they are frequently misunderstood.

**Nepal** has a long-standing open arrangement with India. Indian nationals generally do **not** require a tourist visa to enter Nepal and can typically travel with accepted valid identification rather than a visa. This makes Nepal effectively a long-stay, low-friction destination for Indians — but "no tourist visa" is not the same as "no rules." The accepted documents and any conditions can change, so confirm the current entry requirements before you go rather than assuming.

**Bhutan** also has a special arrangement: Indian nationals generally do not need a standard visa, but entry is managed through a permit system and, importantly, Bhutan levies a **Sustainable Development Fee (SDF)** on visitors. So while the entry route is privileged compared with most nationalities, it is neither free nor unregulated. Check the current permit process and the applicable SDF before travelling — these have been revised in recent years.

> **Key takeaway:** For Indians, Nepal and Bhutan are the closest thing to a long, easy stay — but Nepal's route runs on accepted ID, and Bhutan's runs on a permit plus a Sustainable Development Fee. Confirm the current rules; "special arrangement" never means "no conditions."

## Overstay Risk: Don't Push the Limit

A generous permitted stay is a ceiling, not a target. Overstaying — even by a day — can mean fines, detention, deportation, or bans that follow your passport into future applications elsewhere. Border officers may also grant *less* than the maximum at their discretion, so never assume you'll automatically get the full 90 or 60 days just because the table says so. Note your exact permitted-until date from your stamp or approval, and plan your exit with buffer. If you want to stay longer than the limit, that is a different, longer visa process — not something to improvise at the border.
