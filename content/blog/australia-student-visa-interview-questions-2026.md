## Overview

Australia's student visa (subclass 500) is assessed against the **Genuine Student (GS)** requirement, and applicants — including many from India — can be interviewed to confirm it. The officer is testing whether you genuinely intend to study, whether your choices make sense, whether you can fund it, and whether your plans are credible. A specific, honest, well-prepared performance is what satisfies the GS test.

This guide covers the common Australia student-visa interview questions in 2026, what's assessed, and how to answer. On VisitPlane, we verify every route against official sources, and our [interview prep tool](/interview-prep) lets you rehearse.

> **Key takeaway:** Australia's GS assessment tests whether you're a **genuine student** with credible choices, funding, and plans. Know your course and provider, keep funding answers consistent with your evidence, and explain your decisions clearly and honestly.

## Questions About Your Course and Provider

**"Why do you want to study in Australia?"** — Specific reasons: course strength, employability, lifestyle, or research.

**"Why this institution and course?"** — Reference the curriculum, reputation, or specialisation that fits your goals.

**"What subjects/units will you study?"** — Know your course structure; a key genuineness check.

**"How does this course relate to your previous studies or work?"** — Show a logical progression.

**"Why not study this in India?"** — Positive Australia-specific reasons rather than criticism of home.

## Questions About Funding

**"How will you fund your studies and living costs?"** — Consistent with your evidence. *Sample:* "My parents are funding tuition; I have savings and an education loan covering the living-cost requirement."

**"What is the total cost, including living expenses?"** — Know tuition plus the benchmark (~AUD 29,710/year).

**"Who is your sponsor and what do they do?"** — Be precise and consistent.

**"Do you have OSHC?"** — Confirm your health cover for the full period.

## Questions About Plans and Genuineness

**"What are your plans after you finish?"** — A credible career path; you can mention the 485 visa, but show genuine study intent.

**"Do you intend to work while studying?"** — Acknowledge the cap (48 hours/fortnight in term) and that studies come first.

**"Do you have family in Australia?"** — Answer honestly.

## More Questions You Might Be Asked

**"Tell me about your academic and work background."** — Summarise it briefly.

**"What is your English test score?"** — Know your IELTS/PTE result.

**"Where will you live?"** — Show you've considered accommodation.

**"How did you research this course and provider?"** — Evidence of a deliberate choice.

**"Why this city/state?"** — A specific, honest reason.

**"What will you do if refused?"** — Stay positive and genuine.

## A Sample Exchange (Mock Transcript)

*Officer:* "Why this course and university?"
*You:* "I want to specialise in renewable energy engineering; this programme has strong research facilities and industry placements in [state]."
*Officer:* "How are you funding it?"
*You:* "My parents cover tuition; I have savings and an education loan of [amount] for living costs, with OSHC arranged — all in my documents."
*Officer:* "Plans after graduating?"
*You:* "Use the Temporary Graduate visa to gain experience in renewables, a field growing strongly back home too."

Specific course knowledge, documented funding, and a coherent plan are exactly what the GS assessment rewards.

## How to Answer Well

- **Know your course and provider** in detail.
- **Keep funding answers consistent** with your evidence and OSHC.
- **Explain your choices** with specific, positive reasons.
- **Be honest** about background, scores, and plans.
- **Speak naturally** — GS is a genuineness check, not a memory test.

## What the Officer Is Really Testing

The whole interview feeds the **Genuine Student** judgement: are your choices, funding, and plans those of a real student? Detailed course knowledge, credible documented funding, and a coherent career story answer "yes"; vague or inconsistent answers answer "no."

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Not knowing your course** structure or provider specifics.
- **Funding answers** that contradict your evidence.
- **Generic reasons** for choosing Australia.
- **Overstating work intentions** rather than study.
- **A scripted delivery** that fails under follow-up.

## Strong Answers vs Red-Flag Answers

The same question can help or hurt you depending on how you answer it. A few contrasts make the difference clear.

*"Why did you choose this?"* — **Red flag:** "It's famous / everyone goes there." **Strong:** a specific, personal reason tied to the programme, your background, or your goals. Generic praise signals you haven't really decided; specifics signal a genuine choice.

*"How will you fund it?"* — **Red flag:** a vague "my family will manage" with no figures. **Strong:** named sponsor, relationship, amounts, and instruments (savings, loan, GIC) that match your documents. Precision reads as truth.

*"Will you return / what are your plans?"* — **Red flag:** an over-rehearsed speech or, worse, hints that staying on is the real goal. **Strong:** a calm, concrete plan that fits your background and circumstances.

*Any question* — **Red flag:** long, winding answers that volunteer doubts. **Strong:** one or two sentences, then stop. The Australia Genuine Student interview is testing whether you're a genuine student, and the applicants who do best simply answer that, briefly and honestly, without contradicting their own paperwork.

## The Week Before Your Interview

A short, deliberate run-up makes all the difference. In the final week, re-read your **entire application** — form, financial evidence, and (for students) your course details — so nothing in it can surprise you. Confirm your **key figures** (income, costs, scores, sponsor details) and check they match your documents exactly. **Rehearse aloud**, ideally with someone playing the officer, focusing on your course, funding, and OSHC; practise until your answers feel natural rather than memorised. Organise your **documents** so you can find any of them in seconds. Sort out **logistics** — the location, timing, and what you can bring. The day before, get a good night's sleep and lay out everything you need. Walking in rested, organised, and rehearsed converts nervous energy into the calm composure that officers read as credibility — and it's entirely within your control.

## After the Interview: What to Expect

Once the questions end, the outcome usually follows quickly. In some interviews — notably the US — the officer tells you the decision on the spot: an approval often means handing back your passport for the visa to be stamped and returned, while a refusal is explained briefly, sometimes with a printed notice of the reason. In document-led routes (Schengen, UK, Canada, Australia), the interview or counter questions are just one input, and the formal decision arrives later by email or when your passport is returned through the visa centre.

If you're approved, check the visa details — name, validity, entry type — as soon as you receive it, and only then convert any refundable bookings into paid ones. If you're refused, resist the urge to despair or argue: read the reason carefully, request any available notes, fix the specific weakness, and reapply with a stronger file rather than resubmitting the same one. Either way, stay courteous as you leave; the interview is a professional assessment, not a personal verdict. Knowing what comes next removes much of the anxiety — you walk out understanding the process rather than guessing at it.

## The Bottom Line

Australia's Genuine Student assessment — whether tested by interview or through your written statement — rewards exactly one thing: a real student who made deliberate, fundable choices. The applicants who pass know their course and provider, can explain why they chose them, and give funding answers that match their evidence and OSHC. Re-read your study plan until it's natural, know your numbers, and present a coherent career story that makes your study intent obvious. Speak honestly and specifically, and the GS test becomes a confirmation of what your documents already say rather than an obstacle.
