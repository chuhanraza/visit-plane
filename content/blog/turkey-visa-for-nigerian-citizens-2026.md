## Quick Answer

Nigerian passport holders **cannot use Turkey's standard e-Visa**. There is, however, a **conditional e-Visa**: if you hold a **valid visa or residence permit from a Schengen country, the USA, the UK or Ireland**, you may qualify for a single-entry, 30-day e-Visa via the official portal [evisa.gov.tr](https://www.evisa.gov.tr/en/) — the fee for Nigerians is approximately **USD 60 (roughly NGN 90,000 at mid-2026 rates)**. Everyone else needs a **sticker visa** through the Turkish Embassy in Abuja or Consulate General in Lagos, typically costing around **USD 250** all told and taking **15–21 working days**. ## The Two Routes, Honestly Explained

Turkey runs one of the world's largest e-Visa systems, but eligibility is split into tiers. Some nationalities get an unconditional e-Visa; Nigeria sits in the **conditional tier**, where the e-Visa is available only to applicants who already hold strong third-country documents. That single fact explains most of the confusion online — both "Nigerians can get a Turkey e-Visa" and "Nigerians can't get a Turkey e-Visa" are half-true, depending on what else is in your passport.

So your first step is a triage question: **do you hold a valid (not expired, not electronic-only) visa or residence permit from a Schengen state, the USA, the UK or Ireland?**

- **Yes** → you may qualify for the conditional e-Visa. Fast, online, ~USD 60.
- **No** → you need the sticker visa via the embassy or consulate. Slower, pricier, more documents.

> **Key takeaway:** The Turkey e-Visa is only open to Nigerians who already hold a valid Schengen, US, UK or Irish visa or residence permit. Without one, budget for the embassy sticker-visa route — in both money and weeks.

## Route 1: The Conditional e-Visa

If you qualify, the conditional e-Visa is applied for entirely online at the **official portal only** — [evisa.gov.tr](https://www.evisa.gov.tr/en/). Third-party "e-visa" websites charge markups for the same form, and some are outright scams.

Conditions attached to the conditional e-Visa (as published on the official portal — confirm at application time, since terms are updated):

- A **valid visa or residence permit** from a Schengen country, the USA, the UK or Ireland. E-visas and expired documents generally do not satisfy this condition.
- Travel with an **approved carrier** — Turkish Airlines and certain other airlines with agreements under the scheme.
- A **hotel reservation** and evidence of **sufficient funds** (the portal has cited an indicative amount in the region of USD 50 per day) may be required at the border.
- The visa is **single entry**, valid for stays of up to **30 days** for tourism or business.

The fee for Nigerian applicants is approximately **USD 60** (mid-2026 — the portal shows the exact figure for your nationality before payment), paid by card at the end of the application. Approval is often quick — commonly within minutes to 24 hours — and the e-Visa arrives as a PDF you print and carry. Note that the border officer will want to see the *same supporting visa/permit* you used to qualify, so it must still be valid on your travel date.

### Applying for the Conditional e-Visa, Step by Step

1. Go to [evisa.gov.tr](https://www.evisa.gov.tr/en/) — bookmark it and type it yourself rather than clicking ads.
2. Select Nigeria as your nationality and your travel document type; the portal immediately shows the conditions and exact fee that apply to you.
3. Enter your travel date. The e-Visa's validity window starts from the date you choose, so align it with your actual flight.
4. Declare your supporting document (the valid Schengen/US/UK/Irish visa or residence permit) exactly as it appears — number, type and expiry.
5. Pay by card, download the PDF, and print at least two copies.
6. At check-in and at the Turkish border, carry the e-Visa *and* the supporting visa or permit together — one without the other is a boarding refusal waiting to happen.

If the portal rejects your details at step 4, don't force it with creative answers — it means you don't meet the conditions, and the sticker route below is your path.

## Route 2: The Sticker Visa via the Embassy

Most Nigerian applicants — anyone without a qualifying Schengen/US/UK/Irish document — apply for a traditional **sticker visa** through the Turkish Embassy in **Abuja** or the Consulate General in **Lagos**, with appointments and biometrics handled via the official application channels. Processing typically takes **15–21 working days**, but appointment scarcity is the hidden delay: slots in Lagos and Abuja can book out weeks ahead in peak season. Apply **4–6 weeks before travel** at minimum. Stays granted are commonly 30, 60 or 90 days depending on your case.

### Documents for the Sticker Visa

- International passport valid at least six months beyond your trip, with blank pages.
- Completed application form and biometric photographs.
- **Flight reservation** (not necessarily a paid ticket — keep it refundable) and **hotel bookings** for the full stay.
- **Bank statements** — usually the last 3–6 months, showing stable funds consistent with your trip budget.
- **Employment letter**, or CAC registration and business records for the self-employed; students bring enrolment letters.
- **Travel insurance** valid in Turkey for the whole stay.
- Where available, evidence of ties to Nigeria — previous travel history, family, property or an ongoing job — strengthens the file even when not on the formal list.

The consulate can request additional documents or an interview at its discretion; incomplete files are the most common self-inflicted delay.

## Common Refusal Reasons — and How to Avoid Them

- **Not meeting the conditional e-Visa terms** — applying with an expired or electronic supporting visa, or flying a non-approved carrier. If in doubt, treat yourself as a sticker-visa applicant.
- **Thin financials** — statements that don't plausibly cover the trip, or sudden deposits that look borrowed for the application.
- **Inconsistent trip story** — hotel dates that don't match flights, or a stated purpose the documents don't support.
- **Poor travel history signals** — prior overstays anywhere, especially in Turkey or Schengen, weigh heavily.
- **Unofficial intermediaries** — files lodged through unregistered agents, or e-Visa applications made on copycat websites.

A refusal isn't permanent. Identify the weakness, rebuild the file, and reapply — ideally with stronger funds evidence and a cleaner itinerary.

## What to Expect at the Turkish Border

Approval — e-Visa or sticker — is permission to *ask* for entry, not a guarantee of it. Officers at Istanbul, Antalya and other ports can ask any traveller to show a return ticket, hotel confirmations and means of support, and conditional e-Visa holders should expect exactly those checks, plus inspection of the supporting visa or permit used to qualify. Answer plainly, keep documents in one folder, and make sure the address you give matches your bookings. Respect the granted stay to the day: Turkish overstay penalties combine fines with potential entry bans, and a Turkish overstay is visible baggage on future Schengen and UK applications too.

## Is Turkey Worth It on a Nigerian Budget?

Once past the visa, Turkey is genuinely affordable. Istanbul's public transport is cheap and excellent, lokanta meals cost a fraction of European equivalents, and the lira exchange rate has favoured foreign-currency travellers for years. A comfortable budget traveller can run Istanbul on USD 40–60 a day outside the tourist traps; Cappadocia and the Aegean coast cost more in season. The honest math: for conditional e-Visa holders the entry cost is minor and Turkey competes with the best-value destinations available to Nigerians; for sticker-visa applicants, spread the ~USD 250 entry cost over a longer stay to make it earn its keep — or compare against the visa-free options in our [15 cheapest countries from Nigeria](/blog/15-cheapest-countries-to-visit-from-nigeria-in-2026) guide first.

Apply early and keep every booking refundable until the visa is in hand.
