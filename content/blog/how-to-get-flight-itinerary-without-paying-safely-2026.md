## The Question Everyone Searches — Answered Honestly

"How do I get a flight itinerary for my visa without paying for a ticket?" is one of the most common questions visa applicants ask, and the internet is full of services happy to sell you a "dummy ticket" as the answer. Some of those are legitimate reservation services. Many sell fabricated PDFs that can quietly wreck your application.

Here's the honest version: **you genuinely can get a real, embassy-acceptable flight document for free or nearly free** — without ever touching a fake. This guide walks through exactly how, and why the legitimate routes are not only safer but usually *cheaper* than the paid "dummy ticket" sellers.

## First, What the Embassy Actually Wants

You do not need a **paid** ticket. Almost every consulate — Schengen, UK, Canada, and others — asks for a **reservation** or **itinerary** that demonstrates intended travel, not a purchased, non-refundable flight. Several systems (the US, UK, Canada) explicitly advise you **not** to buy tickets until your visa is approved.

That single fact is what makes the free routes possible. You are allowed to submit a genuine reservation that you have not fully paid for — you just need it to be **real and verifiable**, not fabricated.

---

## Method 1: Airline 24-Hour Free Hold (Usually Free)

Many airlines let you **hold a fare for 24 hours** at no cost, and US law requires carriers to offer either a 24-hour free hold or free cancellation within 24 hours on tickets booked at least seven days before departure. This creates a **genuine PNR** in the airline's system.

If your document submission or VFS appointment falls within that 24-hour window, this is effectively a free, fully legitimate flight reservation. The catch is timing — the hold is short — so it works best when you're submitting documents immediately.

**How to do it:** book directly on the airline's website, choose "hold" or simply don't complete payment within the free-cancellation window, download the confirmation showing the PNR, and submit it. Verify the PNR appears under "Manage Booking" before you rely on it.

## Method 2: Fully Refundable Fare You Cancel After the Decision

This is the gold standard for safety. Book a **fully refundable fare** (or one inside the airline's 24-hour free-cancellation window), submit the confirmation, and **cancel for a full refund** once the visa is decided.

It is 100% real, fully verifiable, and the only "cost" is briefly tying up the fare amount, which you get back. There's no document that an embassy could possibly object to, because it's an actual booking.

## Method 3: A Legitimate Reservation Service (Small Fee)

Some established travel agencies issue a **genuine, verifiable reservation** — a real PNR valid for one to two weeks — for a small fee (often a few dollars). This is genuinely useful when your dates are still flexible and you don't want to tie up funds in a refundable fare.

The litmus test is non-negotiable: **can you verify the PNR with the airline?** A real reservation service gives you a booking reference that shows up on the airline's "Manage Booking" page. If a vendor can't or won't let you verify the booking, it's a fabricated document — skip it.

## Why Not Just Buy a Cheap "Dummy Ticket"?

Because the cheap fabricated ones are document fraud, and the price is not worth the risk. Embassies **spot-check reservations** — some, like Germany and the Netherlands, verify aggressively. A fake PNR that fails verification doesn't just get ignored; it can get your whole application **refused for misrepresentation**, and in systems like Canada's, trigger a **five-year ban**. We cover exactly how this works in [do embassies verify flight reservations](/blog/do-embassies-verify-flight-reservations).

A genuine reservation costs little or nothing. A fabricated one risks everything. There's no version of the math where the fake wins. And confusingly, the same word — "dummy ticket" — is used for both the legitimate short-hold reservation *and* the fraudulent PDF, which is why so many applicants get caught out. ## Step-by-Step: The Free Route, Start to Finish

1. **Lock your real plan.** Decide intended entry/exit dates and route. A coherent plan matters more than a fancy document.
2. **Pick your method by timing.** Submitting immediately? Use a 24-hour hold or refundable fare. Dates still moving? A short-validity reservation service is more flexible.
3. **Create the booking and verify the PNR.** Confirm it appears on the airline's "Manage Booking" page.
4. **Match everything.** Name exactly as in your passport; dates aligned with your [hotel booking](/blog/hotel-booking-for-visa-application-guide) and stated stay.
5. **Submit, then refund/cancel after the decision.** Book real flights once approved; cancel the refundable reservation if refused.
6. **Don't forget insurance.** Schengen and several destinations require travel-medical cover separately — see [best travel insurance for a Schengen visa](/blog/best-travel-insurance-schengen-visa).

## Timing: Match the Reservation to Your Processing Window

The most common way a *legitimate* free reservation goes wrong isn't fraud — it's **expiry**. A 24-hour hold is worthless if the officer reviews your file three days later and the booking has lapsed. So plan the validity around your actual processing timeline:

- **Submitting documents today / same week?** A 24-hour hold or short reservation is fine.
- **Slow consulate or peak season?** Use a **refundable fare** that stays live for weeks, or a reservation service offering one-to-two-week validity. Schengen processing alone can run 15 days or more in summer — see [how long a Schengen visa takes](/blog/how-long-does-schengen-visa-take).
- **Biometrics appointment weeks away?** Don't create the reservation until close to submission, so it's still active when reviewed.

The goal is simple: the reservation should be **live and verifiable on the day a human looks at it**, not just on the day you booked it.

## How to Vet a Reservation Service (Quick Checklist)

If you choose to pay a small fee for a reservation service, run it through these checks before handing over money:

1. **Can you verify the PNR yourself?** A legitimate service gives you a booking reference that appears on the **airline's own** "Manage Booking" page. This is the single most important test.
2. **Does it name a real airline and route?** Genuine reservations are on actual flights, not vague placeholders.
3. **Is the validity stated clearly?** Real holds expire; a service promising an "indefinitely valid ticket" is a red flag.
4. **Does it avoid fraud language?** Steer clear of anything marketing "fake," "novelty," or "for documentation only" tickets — that's the fabricated kind.
5. **Is the price sane?** A genuine short reservation is a few dollars. Anything charging a heavy premium for an *unverifiable* document is selling risk.

If a vendor fails the first test — you can't verify the PNR with the airline — stop there. That's the line between a legitimate reservation and document fraud, no matter how professional the PDF looks.
