## Overview

Germany is one of the most popular European destinations for Indian travellers — Berlin's history and nightlife, the fairytale castles of Bavaria, Munich's beer halls, the Rhine valley, and the Black Forest. It's also a major business and study hub, which means a steady flow of Indian visitors year-round. To visit, Indian passport holders need a **Schengen short-stay visa (Type C)**, which Germany issues for tourism, family visits, and business.

This guide explains the 2026 process for Indian citizens — the new fee, the documents, where you apply, how long it takes, and the tips that separate smooth approvals from frustrating delays. On VisitPlane, we verify every route against official sources.

> **Key takeaway:** Indians need a **Schengen visa** for Germany. Apply through Germany's official partner (**VFS Global**) up to **6 months** before travel; the visa fee rose to **€90** on 11 June 2026, and a decision usually takes **about 15 days** (Germany is among the faster Schengen processors).

## Do Indian Citizens Need a Visa for Germany?

Yes. India is not on the EU visa-exempt list, so Indian passport holders must obtain a Schengen visa before travelling. There is no visa-on-arrival. For tourism or visiting friends and family, you apply for a **short-stay Type C visa** that allows up to **90 days within any 180-day period** across the whole Schengen area — not just Germany.

## How the Schengen Visa Works

- It is a **short-stay visa** valid for the entire **29-country Schengen area**.
- You must apply at the consulate of your **main destination** — if Germany is where you'll spend the most days, you apply to Germany.
- Stays are capped at **90 days in any rolling 180-day window**.
- It can be single- or multiple-entry depending on your travel history and what the consulate grants.

> **Key takeaway:** Apply to **Germany** only if it's your main destination (most nights) or your first point of entry; applying to the wrong country is a common reason for refusal.

## Documents You Need

For a German Schengen tourist visa, prepare:

- A **passport** valid at least **3 months beyond** your planned departure, with two blank pages and issued within the last 10 years.
- The **completed Schengen application form** and a recent **biometric photo**.
- **Travel medical insurance** covering at least **€30,000** across the Schengen area.
- **Round-trip flight reservation** and **confirmed accommodation** for your whole stay.
- **Proof of funds** — usually personal bank statements for the last 3–6 months, plus salary slips or ITRs.
- A **cover letter** explaining your itinerary and purpose, and proof of **ties to India** (employment letter, leave approval, business papers).

## How to Apply: Step by Step

1. **Confirm Germany is your main destination** and book a tentative itinerary.
2. **Fill the application** on Germany's official visa portal.
3. **Book a VFS Global appointment** in India (Delhi, Mumbai, Bengaluru, Chennai, Kolkata, and other centres).
4. **Attend the appointment** to submit documents and give **biometrics** (fingerprints + photo).
5. **Pay the visa fee** plus the VFS service charge.
6. **Track your application** online and collect your passport when the decision is made.

## Costs and Fees (2026)

- **Schengen visa fee:** **€90** for adults (raised from €80 on 11 June 2026); **€45** for children aged 6–11; free under 6.
- In rupees this is roughly **₹8,500**, plus a **VFS Global service charge** of around **₹2,000**, so budget **₹10,000–₹12,000** all-in per adult before insurance.
- Optional extras (premium lounge, courier, photo) add to the total.

Confirm the current amount on the official portal before paying — the fee is non-refundable even if refused.

## Processing Time

Germany is one of the **faster** Schengen processors, often deciding in **5–15 calendar days**. The legal standard is **15 days**, extendable to 45 days if extra checks are needed. Apply **3–4 weeks ahead** in normal times and **6–8 weeks ahead** for summer (June–August) travel when demand peaks.

## Visa Validity and Stay

A short-stay Schengen visa allows up to **90 days within any 180-day period**. The consulate decides the **validity window** and whether it's single or multiple entry — strong travel history improves your chance of a longer, multiple-entry visa. You cannot work on this visa. Track your days carefully across all Schengen countries.

## Why Applications Get Refused (and How to Avoid It)

Most German Schengen refusals come down to a few avoidable issues:

- **Insufficient or unstable funds** — large last-minute deposits look suspicious.
- **Weak ties to India** — no clear reason to return.
- **Inconsistent itinerary** — flights, hotels, and cover letter don't match.
- **Inadequate insurance** — below the €30,000 minimum.
- **Applying to the wrong consulate** when Germany isn't your main destination.

The fee is non-refundable, so review every field and document before submitting.

## How Much Will the Whole Trip Cost?

Rough 2026 estimates per person for an Indian traveller:

- **Visa:** ~₹10,000–₹12,000 including VFS service.
- **Return flights (India–Germany):** roughly ₹45,000–₹90,000 depending on season and city.
- **Accommodation:** ₹6,000–₹15,000 per night in Berlin or Munich.
- **Daily spend (food, transport, attractions):** ₹4,000–₹9,000 per person.
- **Insurance:** ₹1,500–₹3,000 for a short trip.

A typical 7-day Germany trip can land around **₹1.2–2.5 lakh per person**, with flights and hotels the main variables.

## Application Timeline and Pre-Departure Checklist

Apply about **4–6 weeks ahead** (earlier for summer). Sort your passport, insurance, and bookings first, then book the VFS slot.

- Passport valid 3+ months beyond return, 2 blank pages
- Schengen form completed and signed
- Travel insurance ≥ €30,000
- Flight + hotel reservations for the whole stay
- Bank statements (3–6 months) + income proof
- Cover letter and proof of ties to India
- VFS appointment booked and fee ready

Tick every box and Germany is a smooth, welcoming entry point to Europe.

## Best Time to Visit and Practical Tips

Germany is best from **May to September** for warm weather and long days, and **December** for its famous Christmas markets. Practical notes for Indian travellers:

- **Currency:** the Euro (€); cards are widely accepted, but carry some cash for small shops and markets.
- **Connectivity:** prepaid SIMs and eSIMs are easy; public transport (Deutsche Bahn, U-Bahn) is excellent.
- **Getting around:** trains connect every major city; the Deutschland-Ticket offers cheap regional travel.
- **Food:** vegetarian and halal options are widely available in big cities; Indian restaurants are common.
- **Etiquette:** Germans value punctuality and order; queue properly and validate transit tickets.

## What You Can and Can't Do on This Visa

A Schengen Type C visa is a **short-stay visitor visa** for tourism, family visits, or business meetings, capped at **90 days per 180 days**. You **can** travel freely across all Schengen countries within your validity, but you **cannot** work, study long-term, or stay beyond 90 days. Overstaying risks fines and future bans, so track your days across the whole zone.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Booking non-refundable flights** before the visa is approved.
- **Underfunded or freshly topped-up** bank statements.
- **Insurance below €30,000** or with Schengen gaps.
- **Applying to Germany** when most nights are in another country.
- **Thin cover letter** with no clear itinerary or ties to India.

## What to See in Germany

Most trips begin in **Berlin**, where the Brandenburg Gate, Reichstag, Museum Island, and remnants of the Berlin Wall tell the story of modern Europe. **Munich** offers Bavarian charm, beer gardens, and easy access to **Neuschwanstein Castle** — the fairytale palace that inspired Disney. The **Romantic Road** winds through medieval towns like Rothenburg, while **Cologne**'s soaring cathedral and the scenic **Rhine Valley** with its vineyards and castles make memorable additions.

Nature lovers head to the **Black Forest** for hikes and cuckoo-clock villages, while **Hamburg** brings a maritime, modern energy with its harbour and concert hall. For Indian travellers, distances are short, trains are reliable, and a week comfortably covers Berlin or Munich plus two or three day trips. Halal and vegetarian food is easy to find in every major city.

## How to Strengthen Your Germany Application

Whether your route is straightforward or strict, the same principles separate smooth approvals from frustrating delays — and they are worth understanding before you apply. At VisitPlane, we've mapped these patterns across dozens of visa routes, and the advice below applies directly to Indian travellers heading to Germany.

Start with your **finances**, because they are the single most scrutinised part of almost any application. Officers are not simply checking that you have money today; they are checking that your funds are *genuine and stable*. A balance that has sat comfortably in your account for three to six months tells a far more convincing story than a large sum that appeared a week before you applied. If a relative is sponsoring your trip, document that relationship clearly rather than leaving an unexplained deposit.

Next, think about **consistency across your documents**. Your flight dates, hotel bookings, cover letter, and bank statements should all tell one coherent story. If your itinerary says ten days but your hotel booking covers four, that mismatch invites questions and slows everything down. Before you submit, lay your documents side by side and read them as an officer would — every date and detail should line up.

Because the Schengen visa assesses intent, **ties to India** matter enormously. Evidence of stable employment, a running business, property, or dependents reassures the decision-maker that you intend to return after your visit. This is less about any single document and more about the overall picture your application paints.

Finally, be **honest and complete**. Declaring a previous refusal — for any country — is almost always better than hiding it, because non-disclosure is treated far more seriously than an old rejection. Submit every requested document, double-check every field, and apply with enough time to absorb processing and any follow-up requests.
