## Overview

When you're visiting family or friends abroad, an **invitation letter** from your host is one of the most important supporting documents. It tells the visa officer who you're visiting, why, where you'll stay, and who's responsible for what — answering several of their key questions in one document. A vague or thin invitation weakens an otherwise strong file; a complete, well-evidenced one strengthens it.

This guide explains how to write an invitation letter that works in 2026, what to include, who should write it, and the mistakes that cause problems. On VisitPlane, we verify every route against official sources.

> **Key takeaway:** A strong invitation letter clearly states the **host's identity and status, the relationship, the visit dates and purpose, the accommodation, and who covers costs** — backed by the host's supporting documents. Detail and evidence beat warmth and length.

## What an Invitation Letter Is For

The letter does three jobs: it **confirms the purpose** of your trip (visiting a specific person for a specific reason), **shows where you'll stay** (reducing accommodation uncertainty), and **clarifies funding** (whether you or the host pays). Officers use it to test whether the trip is genuine and well-planned.

## Who Writes It

The **host** — your relative or friend in the destination country — writes and signs the letter. They should be a **citizen, permanent resident, or legal visa holder** there, and their status matters: an officer will weigh an invitation from a settled citizen differently from one from a temporary visitor. The host's supporting documents are as important as the letter itself.

## What to Include

A complete invitation letter should contain:

- **Host's full details:** name, address, phone, email, and immigration status (with proof — passport, residence permit, or citizenship).
- **Your details:** full name (as per passport), passport number, date of birth, and your relationship to the host.
- **Purpose of the visit:** why you're coming (family visit, event, holiday together).
- **Exact dates:** arrival and departure, matching your travel plans.
- **Accommodation:** confirmation that you'll stay with the host (with their address) or details of other arrangements.
- **Financial responsibility:** who pays for what — your own funds, the host's support, or both.
- **Signature and date.**

## Supporting Documents the Host Should Attach

The letter alone isn't enough — the host should include:

- **Proof of status** (citizenship, PR card, or visa/residence permit).
- **Proof of address** (utility bill, tenancy, or property document).
- **Proof of funds** if they're sponsoring you (bank statements, payslips).
- Sometimes proof of the **relationship** (family documents, photos, prior correspondence).

## A Simple Structure to Follow

Keep it clear and factual. Open with the host introducing themselves and their status; state who they're inviting and the relationship; give the purpose and exact dates; confirm accommodation and financial responsibility; close with a polite statement that the visitor will return home as planned, then sign and date. A page is plenty — clarity matters more than length.

## Country Nuances

- **Schengen:** some countries use a formal **"formal obligation"/sponsorship form** (for example, the Netherlands and Germany have official sponsorship/obligation documents) in addition to or instead of a free-form letter — check the consulate.
- **UK:** an invitation supports the "genuine visitor" case; the host's status and finances are weighed.
- **US:** an invitation/affidavit of support can help, but the **interview and your own ties** still decide a B1/B2.
- **Canada:** a strong invitation plus host documents supports the purpose-of-visit case; a formal **invitation letter** is commonly requested.

Always check whether your route uses a specific official form rather than a free-form letter.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **A vague letter** with no dates, no address, or no statement on funding.
- **No proof of the host's status** or finances attached.
- **Dates that don't match** your flight and hotel plans.
- **Relying on the invitation alone** while neglecting your own funds and ties.
- **Using the wrong format** when the country requires an official sponsorship form.

## How This Fits Your Wider Application

An invitation letter supports — but doesn't replace — your own evidence. You still need to show **genuine funds, ties to your home country, and a consistent itinerary**. The invitation answers "where and why"; your own documents answer "can you afford it" and "will you return". Together they tell one coherent story.

## Sample Invitation Letter (Adapt This)

Use this as a starting point, adapting every detail to your real situation:

*"To the Visa Officer,*

*I, [Host full name], a [citizen / permanent resident] of [country] (passport/ID no. [number], address [full address]), am writing to invite my [relationship, e.g. brother], [Applicant full name] (passport no. [number], date of birth [DOB]), to visit me from [arrival date] to [departure date].*

*The purpose of the visit is [family visit / specific occasion]. During the stay, [he/she] will reside with me at the above address. I will be responsible for [accommodation and/or living costs], and [the applicant] will fund [their own flights / remaining costs].*

*[The applicant] will return to [home country] at the end of the visit as planned. I have attached proof of my status, address, and finances. Please contact me at [phone/email] for any verification.*

*Sincerely, [Signature, name, date]"*

Keep it factual, one page, and back every claim with the host's documents.

## How the Host Strengthens the Case

The letter is only as strong as the **evidence behind it**. A host who attaches proof of citizenship or residence, a utility bill or tenancy showing the address, and — if they're sponsoring — bank statements and payslips, transforms a friendly note into credible evidence. The host's **status matters**: an invitation from a settled citizen or permanent resident carries more weight than one from someone on a temporary visa. Where the host is also the sponsor, the officer will assess *their* finances, so those documents must be genuine and stable, just like an applicant's own.

## When an Official Form Replaces the Letter

Some countries don't want a free-form letter at all — they require an **official obligation or sponsorship form** completed and sometimes registered by the host with local authorities. Germany's *Verpflichtungserklärung* and the Netherlands' sponsorship/guarantee form are well-known examples. Submitting a casual letter when the consulate expects the official form is a needless cause of delay. Before your host writes anything, check the destination's exact requirement so they prepare the right document the first time — and, if registration is needed, allow time for it.

## The Bottom Line

An invitation letter is one of the most useful supporting documents you can include when visiting family or friends — but only when it's **specific, complete, and backed by the host's evidence**. A warm, vague note that omits dates, the host's status, or who's paying does little; a precise letter naming the relationship, the exact dates, the accommodation, and the funding, attached to the host's proof of status and finances, genuinely strengthens your case. It answers the officer's "where and why" questions before they're asked.

Remember two things above all. First, the invitation **supports but never replaces your own evidence** — you still need genuine funds, ties to home, and a consistent itinerary, because the officer also needs to believe you'll return. Second, **check whether your destination wants an official form** rather than a free-form letter; several Schengen countries require a registered obligation document, and using the wrong format causes needless delay. Prepare the letter and the host's documents early, keep every date aligned with the rest of your file, and the invitation becomes a clear, credible pillar of a coherent application.
