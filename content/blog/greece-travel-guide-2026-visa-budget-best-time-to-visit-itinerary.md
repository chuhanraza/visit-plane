## Visa: What You Need

Greece is in the **Schengen Area**, so most visitors use a Schengen visa or visa-free entry depending on nationality. Entry rules change, so confirm your specific requirement before you book non-refundable travel. Our [Visa Wizard](/wizard) checks the current rules for your nationality in seconds, and our [document checklist](/checklist) helps you prepare anything you need to apply.

## Best Time to Visit

The best time is late spring (May–June) and early autumn (September–October). That window gives you the most reliable conditions and the country at its most appealing, though peak periods bring higher prices and bigger crowds. If you prefer value and space, the shoulder months are a smart trade-off — just check what that means for seasonal openings and daylight.

## Budget: What to Expect

Greece is mid-range — island ferries and tavernas are good value, though peak-summer island prices climb steeply. As always, your biggest levers are when you travel, how far from the centre you stay, and how you eat. Mixing a few memorable splurges with everyday local spots keeps Greece affordable without feeling like you're missing out.

## Top Sights

The highlights are hard to beat: the Acropolis, Santorini's caldera, the beaches of the Cyclades, ancient Delphi, and the old town of Rhodes. For a first visit, build your trip around a handful of these rather than trying to see everything — the country rewards travellers who leave room to slow down and wander.

## A Sample Itinerary

Here's a one-week framework you can adapt:

**Days 1–3 Athens:** the Acropolis, ancient agora, and Plaka. **Days 4–6 Santorini:** caldera views and sunsets. **Days 7–9 a second island** such as Mykonos, Naxos, or Crete. Slower travellers can add Delphi or the Peloponnese.

Treat this as a skeleton, not a script. Add or drop days to match your interests and pace, and build in slower afternoons — the best memories rarely come from the most tightly packed schedules.

## Getting Around

Plan your internal travel as carefully as your arrival. Greece rewards travellers who understand how to move between regions and who allow realistic time for each hop. Sort out transport early — a rail pass, a transit card, or domestic flights as appropriate — and keep digital and paper copies of your bookings. Respect the distances: a relaxed plan that accounts for travel time almost always beats an over-ambitious one.

## Money and Connectivity

A few practical basics smooth everything. Sort out **mobile data** on arrival — a local SIM or eSIM is usually cheap and makes maps, transport, and translation effortless. Carry **a little cash** for small vendors even where cards are widely accepted, and check whether your bank charges foreign-transaction fees. Keep **copies of your passport, visa, and bookings** stored offline and in the cloud.

## Best Time vs Cheapest Time

There's often a gap between the *best* time to visit Greece and the *cheapest*. Peak season brings the finest weather and liveliest atmosphere, but also the highest prices and biggest crowds; the quieter months trade some of that for real savings and breathing room. If your dates are flexible, the shoulder weeks on either side of peak season are frequently the sweet spot. Whichever you choose, book earlier for peak and festival periods, and keep bookings flexible until your visa is confirmed.

## Staying Safe and Respectful

Greece is a welcoming destination, and a little awareness goes a long way. Keep an eye on your belongings in crowded tourist areas, use official or reputable transport, and be mindful of local customs — dressing appropriately at religious or cultural sites and following posted etiquette. Learning a few words of the local language is always appreciated and often opens doors.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Over-scheduling** and spending the trip in transit rather than enjoying it.
- **Underestimating distances** and travel time between regions.
- **Visiting in peak season unaware** of higher prices and crowds.
- **Leaving the visa until after booking** non-refundable travel.
- **Eating only at tourist-facing spots** and missing the best local food.

## How Many Days Do You Need?

A satisfying first visit to Greece usually takes **7 to 10 days** — enough to pair the headline highlights with one or two quieter regions without rushing. If you only have a long weekend, focus on a single city or area and save the rest for next time; trying to cram the whole country into a few days means spending the trip in transit. Travellers with **two weeks or more** can add a second region or simply slow the pace, which is often where the best memories come from. Match the length to your season too, since shorter days or seasonal weather reward a more relaxed plan.

## Where to Stay

Choosing the right bases shapes your whole trip. In Greece, anchor yourself in the main hubs from the itinerary above and take day trips out, rather than changing hotels constantly — fewer moves means less time lost to packing and transfers. As a rule, staying a little outside the most central, sought-after streets cuts your accommodation cost noticeably while keeping you within easy reach by public transport. Decide what you want most from each base — sightseeing convenience, atmosphere, or value — and book the neighbourhoods that deliver it.

## Packing and Practicalities

Pack for Greece's season and your planned activities rather than a generic holiday wardrobe — comfortable walking shoes are essential, and a small daypack, a refillable water bottle, and the right power adapter all earn their place. Build in a buffer for jet lag or altitude on the first day rather than front-loading your must-sees. Carry the originals of any important travel documents plus digital copies, and keep a little local currency for places that don't take cards.

## Plan in the Right Order

The golden rule for a smooth trip: **decide, confirm the visa, then book.** Decide your dates and rough route, confirm your **visa requirement for Greece** with our [Visa Wizard](/wizard), and only then commit to flights and accommodation — keeping them refundable until any visa is secured. Travellers who book first and check the visa later are the ones who risk losing deposits to a refusal or a slow process. Reverse the order and the whole trip gets calmer and often cheaper.

## Making the Most of Your Trip

The travellers who enjoy Greece most aren't the ones who tick off the longest list — they're the ones who balance the famous sights with time to simply *be* in a place: lingering over a meal, wandering a neighbourhood, or sitting somewhere with a view. Pick the experiences you genuinely care about, book the ones that sell out ahead of time, and leave the hours around them loose. That rhythm — a planned highlight plus open time around it — is what turns a sightseeing sprint into a trip you'll remember, and it leaves room for the unexpected discoveries that so often become the best part of the journey.

## The Bottom Line

Greece rewards a little planning and a relaxed pace. Go in late spring (May–June) and early autumn (September–October), sort your visa first, build your days around a few standout sights, use the sample itinerary as a starting point, and keep some hours loose. Do that, and you'll come home with the trip you hoped for — and a long list of reasons to return.
