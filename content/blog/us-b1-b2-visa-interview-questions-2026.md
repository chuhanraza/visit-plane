## Overview

For Indian travellers, the US B1/B2 visitor visa hinges on a **brief consular interview** — frequently under two minutes. Under Section 214(b), the officer presumes you intend to immigrate until you prove otherwise, so almost every question is really probing one thing: do you have **strong ties to India** and a **genuine, temporary purpose** that means you'll return? Knowing the common questions and answering with calm, honest brevity is how you satisfy that test.

This guide covers the most common B1/B2 interview questions in 2026, the intent behind them, and how to answer well. On VisitPlane, we verify every route against official sources, and our [interview prep tool](/interview-prep) lets you rehearse.

> **Key takeaway:** B1/B2 officers test **non-immigrant intent** — strong ties to India and a credible, temporary trip. Answer briefly and honestly, keep everything consistent with your DS-160, and make your reason to return unmistakable.

## Questions About Your Trip

**"What is the purpose of your trip?"** — State it in one clear line. *Sample:* "Tourism — I'm visiting New York and Washington for ten days."

**"How long will you stay?"** — Give a specific, reasonable duration that matches your plan.

**"Have you been to the US before?"** — Answer truthfully, mentioning prior visits and that you returned on time.

**"Who are you travelling with?"** — Be straightforward about companions.

**"Where will you stay?"** — Name the city/hotel or host; it shows planning.

## Questions About Funding

**"Who is paying for your trip?"** — If self-funded, say so; if sponsored, name the sponsor and relationship. Keep it consistent with your finances.

**"What do you do for a living, and what's your income?"** — Know your role and salary; this is core ties evidence.

**"How much will the trip cost?"** — A rough, realistic figure shows you've planned and can afford it.

## Questions About Ties to India

**"Do you have family in the US?"** — Answer honestly. Relatives there aren't disqualifying, but be clear about your own ties at home.

**"Are you married? Do you have children?"** — Family responsibilities at home are strong ties; state them plainly.

**"What ties do you have to India?"** — This is the heart of 214(b). Mention your job, business, property, and family — concrete reasons you'll return.

**"Will you return to India?"** — Reaffirm your ties and plans without sounding rehearsed.

## More Questions You Might Be Asked

**"What is your monthly/annual salary?"** — Know it precisely; it supports both affordability and ties.

**"How long have you worked at your current job?"** — Tenure signals stability.

**"Why do you want to visit now?"** — A genuine reason (holiday, a specific event, visiting family) is fine — just be honest.

**"Do you own property or a business in India?"** — Strong ties; mention them if true.

**"What will you do if your visa is refused?"** — Stay positive: "I'll understand the reason and reapply if my plans allow."

**"Have you applied for a US visa before?"** — Disclose any prior application or refusal truthfully — non-disclosure is far worse than an old refusal.

**"Who will look after your business/job while you're away?"** — A simple, real answer reinforces that you're returning.

## A Sample Two-Minute Interview (Mock Transcript)

*Officer:* "Purpose of your trip?"
*You:* "Tourism — ten days in New York and Washington."
*Officer:* "Who's paying?"
*You:* "I am; I work as a [role] at [company] earning [amount], and I've saved for this trip."
*Officer:* "Any family in the US?"
*You:* "A cousin in New Jersey, but I'm going as a tourist and staying in hotels."
*Officer:* "What keeps you in India?"
*You:* "My job, my wife and two children, and our home in [city] — I'll be back at work on [date]."
*Officer:* "Approved. Enjoy your trip."

Every answer is short, honest, and points back to ties and a temporary purpose — exactly what the officer needs.

## How to Answer Well

- **Be concise** — one or two sentences; don't over-explain.
- **Be honest** — never inflate income or hide relatives or past refusals.
- **Lead with ties** — make your reason to return obvious.
- **Stay calm and friendly** — nervousness can read as evasiveness.
- **Match your DS-160** — consistency is everything.

## What the Officer Is Really Testing

Every question, however casual, feeds the 214(b) judgement: are your ties to India strong enough, and is this trip genuinely temporary? If your answers consistently show a stable life at home — job, family, property — and a credible, time-bound purpose, you've answered the real question regardless of the wording.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Over-talking** and volunteering doubts.
- **Vague ties** — failing to convey what brings you home.
- **Income or funding** that doesn't match your documents.
- **Hiding relatives or a prior refusal** — honesty is essential.
- **A memorised script** that doesn't fit the question asked.

## Strong Answers vs Red-Flag Answers

The same question can help or hurt you depending on how you answer it. A few contrasts make the difference clear.

*"Why did you choose this?"* — **Red flag:** "It's famous / everyone goes there." **Strong:** a specific, personal reason tied to the programme, your background, or your goals. Generic praise signals you haven't really decided; specifics signal a genuine choice.

*"How will you fund it?"* — **Red flag:** a vague "my family will manage" with no figures. **Strong:** named sponsor, relationship, amounts, and instruments (savings, loan, GIC) that match your documents. Precision reads as truth.

*"Will you return / what are your plans?"* — **Red flag:** an over-rehearsed speech or, worse, hints that staying on is the real goal. **Strong:** a calm, concrete plan that fits your background and circumstances.

*Any question* — **Red flag:** long, winding answers that volunteer doubts. **Strong:** one or two sentences, then stop. The B1/B2 visitor interview is testing non-immigrant intent under Section 214(b), and the applicants who do best simply answer that, briefly and honestly, without contradicting their own paperwork.

## The Week Before Your Interview

A short, deliberate run-up makes all the difference. In the final week, re-read your **entire application** — form, financial evidence, and (for students) your course details — so nothing in it can surprise you. Confirm your **key figures** (income, costs, scores, sponsor details) and check they match your documents exactly. **Rehearse aloud**, ideally with someone playing the officer, focusing on your ties to India and a clear, temporary purpose; practise until your answers feel natural rather than memorised. Organise your **documents** so you can find any of them in seconds. Sort out **logistics** — the location, timing, and what you can bring. The day before, get a good night's sleep and lay out everything you need. Walking in rested, organised, and rehearsed converts nervous energy into the calm composure that officers read as credibility — and it's entirely within your control.

## The Bottom Line

The B1/B2 interview is short, but under Section 214(b) it carries the whole decision. Officers aren't hostile — they simply need to be convinced you'll return, and the way to convince them is a stable, honest picture of your life in India plus a clear, temporary purpose. Keep answers to one or two sentences, lead with your ties, stay consistent with your DS-160, and never hide a relative or a past refusal. Prepare the ideas, rehearse until they're natural, and treat it as a brief, friendly conversation — that calm clarity is what approval sounds like.
