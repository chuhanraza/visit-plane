## Overview

Malaysia — Kuala Lumpur's Petronas Towers, the beaches of Langkawi, the rainforests of Borneo, and a food scene that rivals anywhere in Asia — is a top destination for Bangladeshi travellers. Unlike Indian and Chinese nationals (who currently enjoy visa-free entry), **Bangladeshi passport holders need a visa**, most commonly the **Malaysia eVisa**, applied for online.

This guide explains the 2026 process for Bangladeshi citizens — eligibility, the eVisa vs eNTRI options, the mandatory arrival card, documents, fees, and entry tips. On VisitPlane, we verify every route against official sources.

> **Key takeaway:** Bangladeshi citizens need a Malaysia **eVisa** (or eNTRI where eligible), applied for **online** before travel. You must also complete the **Malaysia Digital Arrival Card (MDAC)** before arrival.

## Do Bangladeshi Citizens Need a Visa for Malaysia?

Yes. Bangladesh is not on Malaysia's visa-free list, so you need a visa before you travel. The main options are:

- **eVisa** — a standard electronic visa for tourism, applied for online; typically allows a **30-day** stay.
- **eNTRI** — a simplified electronic travel registration available to certain nationalities for short visits (check current eligibility for Bangladesh before relying on it).

For most Bangladeshi tourists, the **eVisa** is the route to use.

## The Malaysia Digital Arrival Card (MDAC)

Separate from your visa, **every visitor must submit the free MDAC** online, within **3 days before arrival**, on the official Malaysian Immigration portal. Carry the confirmation (digital or printed) to show at immigration.

> **Key takeaway:** A visa is not enough on its own — the **MDAC is compulsory** and must be submitted shortly before you fly.

## Documents You Need

For the Malaysia eVisa, Bangladeshi applicants should prepare:

- A **passport valid for at least 6 months** with blank pages.
- A recent **passport-size photograph**.
- **Confirmed return/onward flights**.
- **Accommodation** bookings.
- **Bank statement** showing sufficient funds.
- Proof of employment or business, and a covering letter where relevant.

> **Key takeaway:** Show **genuine, stable funds** and confirmed onward travel — these are the factors that most influence approval.

## How to Apply: Step by Step

1. **Apply online** for the Malaysia eVisa through the official immigration visa platform.
2. **Upload** your passport, photo, flights, accommodation, and bank statement.
3. **Pay the fee** online.
4. **Receive the eVisa by email** (usually within a few working days) and print it.
5. **Submit the MDAC** within 3 days before arrival.
6. **Arrive in Malaysia**, present your eVisa, MDAC, and supporting documents.

## Costs and Fees (2026)

- **eVisa government fee:** a modest amount plus the portal/agent service charge — typically totalling the equivalent of **BDT 4,000–7,000** depending on the provider.
- **MDAC:** free.

Confirm the current fee on the official platform before paying.

## Processing Time

The Malaysia eVisa is usually processed within a **few working days** (commonly up to about a week). Apply **at least 2 weeks** before travel to be safe.

## Visa Validity and Stay

The eVisa generally allows a **single entry** with a stay of up to **30 days**. You cannot work on it, and overstaying carries fines and possible bans. Track your days from the entry stamp.

## Why Applications Get Refused (and How to Avoid It)

For Bangladeshi applicants, watch for:

- **Insufficient or unstable funds.**
- **Mismatched flights/accommodation** and stated dates.
- **Weak ties to Bangladesh** (employment, business, family).
- **Forgetting the MDAC**, which can cause issues at the border even with a valid visa.

## How Much Will the Whole Trip Cost?

Rough 2026 estimates per person for a Bangladeshi traveller:

- **Visa:** BDT 4,000–7,000 (eVisa); MDAC free.
- **Return flights (Bangladesh–Malaysia):** roughly BDT 45,000–75,000 depending on season.
- **Accommodation:** BDT 3,500–10,000 per night in Kuala Lumpur.
- **Daily spend (food, transport, activities):** BDT 3,500–7,000 per person.
- **Day trips (Langkawi, Genting, Malacca):** modest add-ons.

A typical 6–7 day Malaysia trip can land around **BDT 120,000–220,000 per person**, mostly flights.

## Best Time to Visit and Practical Tips

Malaysia is a year-round destination; the west coast (Kuala Lumpur, Penang, Langkawi) is driest **December–April**, the east coast best **March–October**. Practical notes for Bangladeshi travellers:

- **Currency:** the Malaysian Ringgit (MYR); cards are widely accepted, with cash useful for hawker stalls and small towns.
- **Connectivity:** tourist SIMs are cheap; Grab is the main ride-hailing app.
- **Getting around:** budget flights link Kuala Lumpur with Langkawi, Penang, and Borneo (note Sabah and Sarawak have separate immigration checks).
- **Etiquette:** dress modestly, especially at mosques; Malaysia is Muslim-majority with abundant halal food.
- **MDAC:** remember the arrival card is separate from your visa and must be submitted before arrival.

## What You Can and Can't Do on This Visa

The eVisa is for **tourism and short visits** — typically up to **30 days**, single entry. You can sightsee, island-hop, and visit family. You **cannot** work or run a business on it. Overstaying carries fines and possible bans. If you need longer or a non-tourist purpose, apply for the appropriate visa or pass.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Forgetting the MDAC** (a valid visa alone isn't enough at the border).
- **Weak or unstable funds.**
- **Mismatched bookings** versus stated dates.
- **Assuming visa-free entry** (that applies to India and China, not Bangladesh).
- **Applying too late** — allow at least two weeks.

## How to Strengthen Your Malaysia Application

Whether your route is straightforward or strict, the same principles separate smooth approvals from frustrating delays — and they are worth understanding before you apply. At VisitPlane, we've mapped these patterns across dozens of visa routes, and the advice below applies directly to Bangladeshi travellers heading to Malaysia.

Start with your **finances**, because they are the single most scrutinised part of almost any application. Officers and systems are not simply checking that you have money today; they are checking that your funds are *genuine and stable*. A balance that has sat comfortably in your account for three to six months tells a far more convincing story than a large sum that appeared a week before you applied. If a relative is supporting your trip, document that relationship clearly rather than leaving an unexplained deposit. Keep your statements clean, recent, and consistent with the trip you are describing.

Next, think about **consistency across your documents**. Your flight dates, hotel bookings, cover letter, and bank statements should all tell one coherent story. If your itinerary says ten days but your hotel booking covers four, or your stated purpose is tourism while your documents suggest something else, that mismatch invites questions and slows everything down. Before you submit, lay your documents side by side and read them as an officer would — every date and detail should line up.

For routes that assess intent, **ties to your home country** matter enormously. Evidence of stable employment, a running business, property, or dependents reassures the decision-maker that you intend to return after your visit to Malaysia. This is less about any single document and more about the overall picture your application paints.

Finally, be **honest and complete**. Declaring a previous refusal — for any country — is almost always better than hiding it, because non-disclosure is treated far more seriously than an old rejection. Submit every requested document, double-check every field, and apply with enough time to absorb processing and any follow-up requests.
