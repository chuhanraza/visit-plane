## Overview

Tokyo is a city of dazzling contrasts — ancient temples and hyper-modern districts, Michelin dining and tiny noodle bars, neon nightlife and serene gardens — endlessly rewarding for first-timers and returnees alike. This 2026 guide covers what to see, when to go, what it costs, how to get around, and the visa basics — everything you need to plan a smooth first trip.

> **Key takeaway:** Visit Tokyo ideally in spring cherry-blossom season (late March–April) and autumn (October–November). See the headline sights, eat well for less, use public transport, and sort your visa before booking — and you'll have a brilliant, well-paced trip.

## Top Things to Do

The essentials are the temples of Asakusa, the crossing and energy of Shibuya, the electronics and anime of Akihabara, the gardens of the Imperial Palace, and a day trip to Mount Fuji or Nikko. For a first visit, build your days around two or three of these rather than racing between all of them — Tokyo rewards travellers who leave room to wander and stumble on the unexpected.

## When to Visit

The best time is spring cherry-blossom season (late March–April) and autumn (October–November). That timing gives you the most reliable weather and the city at its most appealing, though you'll share the headline sights with more people in peak periods. If you prefer lower prices and thinner crowds, the quieter months can be a smart trade-off — just check what that means for daylight hours and seasonal openings. Our [best-time guides](/blog) go deeper on the seasons.

## What It Costs

Tokyo is mid-to-high range, but smart travellers eat superbly for little at noodle shops and convenience stores while saving splurges for special meals. As always, your biggest levers are when you travel, how far from the centre you stay, and how you eat. Mixing a few memorable splurges with everyday local spots keeps Tokyo affordable without feeling like you're missing out.

## Getting Around

The vast, punctual rail and metro network is the way around — a rechargeable Suica/PASMO card makes it effortless; the JR Pass helps for wider Japan travel. Sorting out transport on arrival — a transit card, an offline map, and a rough sense of the districts — pays off immediately and saves both money and time over taxis for every hop.

## Food and Drink

Half the joy of Tokyo is eating. Don't miss sushi, ramen, tempura, and izakaya dining, from humble counters to world-famous restaurants. Some of the best meals are the cheapest, so follow the locals, be willing to queue for a popular spot, and try at least one dish you've never heard of.

## A Suggested First-Timer Itinerary

With **three days**, dedicate one to the central headline sights, one to a contrasting neighbourhood plus a museum or cultural site, and one to a day trip or a slower, local-feeling area. With more time, add a second day trip and simply build in space to relax. The most common first-timer mistake is over-scheduling — Tokyo is best enjoyed at a pace that leaves room for a long lunch and an unplanned detour.

## Practical Tips

Carry a little local currency for small purchases, keep digital and paper copies of your bookings and any entry documents, dress appropriately for cultural or religious sites, and build in a buffer for jet lag on day one. A small amount of preparation — a transit card, a couple of restaurant ideas, and a sense of the layout — turns a good trip into a great one.

## Where to Stay

Choosing the right base shapes your whole trip. In Tokyo, consider Shinjuku or Shibuya for transport links and nightlife, Asakusa for traditional atmosphere, or the Tokyo Station/Ginza area for central convenience. As a rule, staying a little outside the most central, sought-after streets cuts your accommodation cost noticeably while keeping you within easy reach by public transport. Decide first what you want most — sightseeing convenience, atmosphere, nightlife, or value — and pick the neighbourhood that delivers it, rather than booking the first central hotel you find.

## Day Trips Worth Taking

If you have more than a couple of days, Tokyo makes a great base for exploring beyond the city. Among the best options are Mount Fuji and the Hakone hot springs, the shrines of Nikko, the temples of Kamakura, or a bullet-train day trip to Kyoto. Day trips are also a smart way to balance an intense city visit with a slower, scenic day — and they often become the highlight of the trip. Check transport times and the first and last departures so you don't cut a day trip short, and carry your essentials in case plans shift.

## Money, Connectivity, and Staying Connected

A few practical basics smooth everything. Sort out **mobile data** on arrival — a local SIM or eSIM is usually cheap and makes maps, transport, and translation effortless. Carry **a little cash** for small vendors even where cards are widely accepted, and check whether your bank charges foreign-transaction fees before you rely on a card. Keep **copies of your passport, visa, and bookings** stored offline and in the cloud. None of this is glamorous, but getting it right on day one means you spend the rest of the trip enjoying Tokyo rather than troubleshooting.

## Best Time vs Cheapest Time

There's often a gap between the *best* time to visit Tokyo and the *cheapest*. Peak season brings the finest weather and the liveliest atmosphere, but also the highest prices and the biggest crowds; the quieter months trade some of that for real savings and breathing room at the sights. If your dates are flexible, the shoulder weeks on either side of peak season are frequently the sweet spot — good conditions, easier availability, and gentler prices. Whichever you choose, book flights and accommodation earlier for peak and festival periods, and keep bookings flexible until your visa for Japan is confirmed.

## Staying Safe and Respectful

Tokyo is a welcoming destination, and a little awareness goes a long way. Keep an eye on your belongings in crowded tourist areas, use official or reputable transport, and be mindful of local customs — dressing appropriately at religious or cultural sites and following posted etiquette. Learning a few words of the local language is always appreciated and often opens doors. Travelling respectfully isn't just courteous; it tends to bring out the best of a place and its people, and it's the surest way to a trip you'll remember warmly.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Over-scheduling** and spending the trip in transit rather than enjoying it.
- **Relying on taxis** instead of Tokyo's public transport.
- **Visiting in peak season unaware** of higher prices and crowds.
- **Eating only at tourist-facing spots** and missing the best local food.
- **Leaving visa checks until after booking** non-refundable travel.

## How Many Days Do You Need?

If you're wondering how long to give Tokyo, a satisfying first visit usually takes **three to five days** for the city itself, plus a day or two if you want to add the day trips above. Three days covers the headline sights at a comfortable pace; five lets you mix famous attractions with quieter neighbourhoods and a slower afternoon or two. Travellers passing through on a tighter schedule can still get a strong taste in **48 hours** by focusing on one or two areas — but if you can spare the time, the extra days are what turn a checklist visit into a real sense of the place.

## The Bottom Line

Tokyo is one of those destinations that rewards a little planning and a relaxed pace. Go in spring cherry-blossom season (late March–April) and autumn (October–November), build your days around a few standout sights, eat where the locals do, lean on public transport, and confirm your visa before you commit to travel. Do that, and you'll come home with the trip you hoped for — and a long list of reasons to return.
