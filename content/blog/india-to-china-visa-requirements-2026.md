## Overview

China — from the Great Wall and the Forbidden City to Shanghai's skyline and the karst landscapes of Guilin — is reopening to Indian travellers, and there has never been a more affordable time to go. **Reduced visa fees remain in effect through 31 December 2026**, making the Chinese **L (tourist) visa** notably cheaper than in previous years.

Indian passport holders do, however, need a **visa in advance** — China does not offer visa on arrival or visa-free entry to Indian tourists. This guide explains the full 2026 process: who applies where, the documents and biometrics required, the current fees, and realistic processing times. > **Key takeaway:** Indians need a pre-approved Chinese visa. Apply through a **Chinese Visa Application Service Centre (CVASC)** — reduced fees apply through 31 December 2026, after which prices may rise.

## Do Indian Citizens Need a Visa for China?

Yes. For tourism you need the **L visa** (tourist), applied for before you travel. There is **no visa on arrival** for Indian nationals for general tourism. Business travellers use the **M visa**, and those visiting relatives use other categories — this guide focuses on the tourist L visa.

## Where Do Indians Apply?

Applications are submitted at a **Chinese Visa Application Service Centre (CVASC)**, with centres in **New Delhi, Mumbai, and Kolkata**. Each centre covers specific regions of India:

- **Delhi** handles most northern and central states.
- **Mumbai** covers Maharashtra and Karnataka.
- **Kolkata** covers Bihar, Jharkhand, Odisha, Chhattisgarh, and West Bengal.

Most applicants must attend in person to give **biometrics (fingerprints)**.

## Documents You Need

For the China L (tourist) visa, Indian applicants should prepare:

- A passport valid for **at least 6 months** with blank pages.
- The completed China visa application form.
- A recent **passport photo** meeting China's specifications.
- **Round-trip flight booking** showing entry and exit dates.
- **Hotel bookings** for your full stay or a detailed itinerary.
- **Bank statements** demonstrating sufficient funds.
- Proof of employment or business, and, in some cases, an invitation letter.

First-time applicants and those visiting friends or relatives may need additional documents such as an invitation.

> **Key takeaway:** A clear, complete itinerary (flights + hotels matching your dates) is central to a smooth China visa approval. Gaps or mismatches trigger requests for more documents.

## How to Apply: Step by Step

1. **Complete the online application form** for the L visa.
2. **Book an appointment** at your designated CVASC (Delhi, Mumbai, or Kolkata).
3. **Attend in person**, submit documents, and give **biometrics**.
4. **Pay the visa fee and service charge** (see below).
5. **Track and collect** your passport, or use courier where available.

## Costs and Fees (2026)

Under the reduced fee structure in effect **through 31 December 2026**, indicative visa fees for Indian applicants are:

- **Single entry:** around ₹2,900
- **Double entry:** around ₹4,400
- **Six-month multiple entry:** around ₹5,900
- **One-year (or longer) multiple entry:** around ₹8,800

On top of the visa fee, **CVASC service charges** apply — roughly **₹1,973** for regular service and **₹2,908** for express. Fees may increase from 2027, so 2026 is a good year to travel.

## Processing Times

Standard processing is typically **4–6 working days** after biometrics. Express options may be available at a higher service charge. Apply at least **2–3 weeks** before travel to allow for any document requests or peak-season delays.

## Visa Validity and Conditions

The L visa's validity, number of entries, and permitted stay per visit are printed on the visa. A common tourist grant allows a stay of up to **30 days per entry**, but this varies by applicant. You cannot work on an L visa. Always read the visa to confirm the **"enter before" date**, number of entries, and **duration of each stay**.

## Tips to Maximise Approval

- **Match flights and hotels to your stated dates** — consistency matters.
- **Show stable funds** over recent months.
- **Apply in 2026** to benefit from the reduced fees.
- **Choose the correct CVASC** for your region and book early.
- **Double-check your visa** on collection for the correct entries and validity.

## How Much Will the Whole Trip Cost?

Budgeting the whole trip helps your application look realistic and keeps you prepared. Rough 2026 estimates per person for an Indian traveller:

- **Visa:** around ₹2,900 (single entry) plus CVASC service charge (~₹1,973 regular).
- **Return flights (India–China):** roughly ₹40,000–₹75,000 depending on city and season.
- **Accommodation:** ₹4,000–₹10,000 per night in major cities; cheaper outside the centre.
- **Daily spend (food, transport, attractions):** ₹3,000–₹6,000 per person.
- **Travel insurance:** ₹1,500–₹3,000 for a typical trip.
- **Internal travel:** China's high-speed rail is excellent value for hopping between cities.

A typical 7-day China trip lands around **₹1–1.7 lakh per person**, and the reduced visa fees through 2026 make it cheaper than recent years. Set up **mobile payments before you go** — much of China is effectively cashless, and having Alipay/WeChat Pay ready will save you real friction on the ground.

## Visiting Family, Business, or Beyond Tourism

This guide focuses on the **L (tourist) visa**, but China issues different categories depending on your purpose:

- **M visa** — for commerce and trade (business meetings, trade fairs). You'll usually need an invitation from a Chinese company or trade body.
- **Q2 visa** — for short visits to relatives who are Chinese citizens or permanent residents; an invitation letter from the relative is required.
- **F visa** — for non-commercial exchanges, visits, or study tours.

Choosing the correct category from the start avoids delays. If your trip mixes tourism with a short business component, ask the CVASC which category fits — applying under the wrong type is a common reason applications are returned.

## Best Time to Visit and Practical Tips

China is vast, so the best time depends on the region, but **spring (April–May)** and **autumn (September–October)** generally offer the most comfortable weather for sightseeing in cities like Beijing, Shanghai, and Xi'an. Summers are hot and humid; winters are cold in the north.

Practical notes for Indian travellers:

- **Go cashless:** China runs largely on mobile payments (Alipay, WeChat Pay). Set these up and link an international card before you travel — cash and foreign cards are accepted in fewer places than you'd expect.
- **Connectivity:** many familiar apps and services are restricted; download maps, translation tools, and a reliable VPN (where lawful) before arrival, and consider a local or roaming SIM.
- **Carry your passport:** hotels, train stations, and many attractions require it for registration and entry.
- **Language:** English is limited outside major hubs; a translation app is invaluable.
- **Apply in 2026** to lock in the reduced visa fees before any 2027 increase.

## Application Timeline and Pre-Departure Checklist

Work backwards from your travel date so biometrics and processing don't catch you out. Standard processing is about 4–6 working days after biometrics. As a rule of thumb, lock your passport validity and bookings first, then complete the visa or entry steps, and keep everything consistent across documents. Plan with a buffer: the most common delay is a flight/hotel itinerary that doesn't match the dates on your form, so handle that early rather than at the last minute.

Use this quick checklist before you go:

- Choose the correct CVASC for your region
- Book a biometrics appointment early
- Flights and hotels matching your stated dates
- Bank statements showing stable funds
- Apply 2–3 weeks ahead (more in peak season)
- Set up Alipay/WeChat Pay before you fly

Tick every box and you remove the vast majority of avoidable delays and refusals. When in doubt, confirm the latest rules on the official source before you travel, since fees and procedures can change.

China rewards travellers who prepare: a clear itinerary, stable funds, and mobile payments set up in advance turn a potentially bureaucratic trip into a smooth one. Get those three right and the visa is rarely the hard part.
