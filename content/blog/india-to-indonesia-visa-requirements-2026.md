## Overview

Indonesia — and Bali in particular — is a top beach-and-culture destination for Indian travellers, and entry is refreshingly simple. Indian passport holders are eligible for a **Visa on Arrival (VoA)** or the online **e-VOA**, granting a **30-day stay** that is **extendable once** for a further 30 days.

There is one newer cost to plan for: Bali now charges a separate **tourist levy** on top of the visa fee. This guide explains the VoA vs e-VOA choice, the exact fees, the Bali levy, how to extend, and the documents you need. On VisitPlane, we verify every route against official sources.

> **Key takeaway:** Indians can enter Indonesia on a **VoA / e-VOA (IDR 500,000, ~₹2,950)** for **30 days**, extendable once. For Bali, also budget the **tourist levy of IDR 150,000 (~₹750)** per person.

## Do Indian Citizens Need a Visa for Indonesia?

You don't need a visa **in advance**, but you do need to obtain a **Visa on Arrival** (paid at the airport) or, more conveniently, an **e-VOA** (paid online before you fly). Both give a single entry for **tourism** with a **30-day** stay.

The **e-VOA** is recommended because it:

- Lets you pay and get approved **before departure**.
- Speeds up your airport arrival.
- Makes a later **extension** easier to process online.

## VoA vs e-VOA

- **Visa on Arrival (VoA):** purchased at the immigration counter on arrival at eligible airports/seaports.
- **e-VOA:** applied for online (ideally at least a few days, and up to 14 days, before travel), with approval emailed to you. You show it at immigration.

Both cost the same government fee; the e-VOA simply removes the airport queue and is easier to extend.

## The Bali Tourist Levy

Separately from your visa, Bali charges a **mandatory tourist levy of IDR 150,000** (about ₹750 / USD 10) **per person, per entry**, applicable to all international visitors regardless of age or visa type. Pay it online before arrival (via Bali's official Love Bali platform) or on arrival, and **keep the QR/receipt** — you may be asked to show it. This levy is specific to Bali; other Indonesian regions may differ.

## Documents and Requirements

For the VoA / e-VOA, Indian travellers need:

- A **passport valid for at least 6 months** from arrival, with a blank page.
- A **return or onward flight** booking.
- Proof of **accommodation**.
- Means to pay the **VoA fee** and **Bali levy**.
- Sufficient **funds** for your stay.

> **Key takeaway:** The VoA is for **tourism only**. You cannot work on it, and the 30-day clock starts on your arrival date — plan your extension before it runs out.

## How to Apply (e-VOA): Step by Step

1. **Apply online** for the e-VOA on Indonesia's official immigration e-VOA portal, ideally a few days before travel.
2. **Upload** your passport bio-page and a photo, and enter your travel details.
3. **Pay** the IDR 500,000 fee online.
4. **Receive approval** by email and save/print it.
5. **Pay the Bali tourist levy** (if visiting Bali) and keep the receipt.
6. **Arrive in Indonesia**, present your e-VOA and passport, and clear immigration.

## Costs (2026)

- **VoA / e-VOA fee:** **IDR 500,000** (~₹2,950 / USD 35), single entry, 30 days.
- **Bali tourist levy:** **IDR 150,000** (~₹750) per person, per entry (Bali only).
- **Extension:** an additional fee if you extend for another 30 days.

## Extending Your Stay

The VoA/e-VOA can be **extended once** for a further **30 days**, giving a maximum of about 60 days. e-VOA holders can usually start the extension online; VoA holders may need to visit a local immigration office. Apply for the extension **before** your initial 30 days expire to avoid overstay fines, which are charged per day.

## Tips for a Smooth Trip

- **Use the e-VOA** to skip the airport queue and ease extensions.
- **Pre-pay the Bali levy** and keep the receipt handy.
- **Carry onward-ticket and accommodation proof** for immigration.
- **Don't overstay** — daily fines apply.
- **Respect local customs**, especially at temples and religious sites.

## What You Can and Can't Do on a VoA

The Visa on Arrival / e-VOA is strictly for **tourism and short visits**. On it you can sightsee, relax on the beaches, visit temples, and travel between islands. You **cannot** work, run a business, or undertake paid activities — including remote work that breaches the tourist purpose. If you plan to stay long-term, study, or work, you need a different visa category (such as the C1 tourist visa for longer stays, or a work/stay permit).

Overstaying is taken seriously: Indonesia charges a **daily overstay fine**, and significant overstays can lead to detention and deportation. Always track your 30 days from the arrival stamp and extend in good time if needed.

## Best Time to Visit and Practical Tips

Bali and much of Indonesia have a **dry season (roughly April–October)** that's ideal for beaches and outdoor activities, and a wetter season from November to March. The July–August and year-end holidays are busiest.

Practical notes for Indian travellers:

- **Currency:** the Indonesian Rupiah (IDR); cards work in hotels and larger venues, but carry cash for warungs (local eateries), markets, and small towns.
- **Pre-pay the Bali levy:** do it online via Love Bali before arrival and keep the QR/receipt.
- **Transport:** ride-hailing apps (Gojek, Grab) are widely used; scooter rental is popular but requires care and a valid licence.
- **Respect customs:** dress modestly at temples (a sarong is often required) and follow etiquette at religious sites and ceremonies.
- **Use the e-VOA:** it speeds up your arrival and makes the 30-day extension far easier than the on-arrival VoA.

## Application Timeline and Pre-Departure Checklist

The e-VOA is quick, but give yourself a small buffer before departure. e-VOA approval typically comes within a few business days. As a rule of thumb, lock your passport validity and bookings first, then complete the visa or entry steps, and keep everything consistent across documents. Plan with a buffer: the most common slip is assuming you can buy a counter VoA — apply for the e-VOA online and pre-pay the Bali levy, so handle that early rather than at the last minute.

Use this quick checklist before you go:

- Passport valid 6+ months with a blank page
- e-VOA applied for online before travel
- Bali tourist levy paid (keep the QR/receipt)
- Return/onward ticket booked
- Accommodation confirmed
- Track your 30 days and extend in time if needed

Tick every box and you remove the vast majority of avoidable delays and refusals. When in doubt, confirm the latest rules on the official source before you travel, since fees and procedures can change.

Indonesia is one of the most forgiving destinations for first-time international travellers from India — but the small admin steps (e-VOA and the Bali levy) are exactly what catch people out, so handle them online before you fly.
